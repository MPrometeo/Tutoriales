## TASK 3: Integrated — Listen to Conversation + Speak (Preparación: 20s | Respuesta: 60s)

**Listening passage (usa TTS):**

> **Professor:** Before we end today, I want to discuss a concept relevant
> to your reading: the tragedy of the commons. This idea, popularized by
> ecologist Garrett Hardin in 1968, describes a situation where individuals
> acting in their own self-interest deplete a shared resource, even though
> it's against everyone's long-term interest.
>
> Let me give you a concrete example. Imagine a small fishing village with
> a lake that naturally produces enough fish to feed everyone, as long as
> each family catches no more than 10 fish per week. The lake can regenerate
> at that rate. But one family decides to catch 20 fish per week to sell
> the extras at the market. Other families see this and think, "If we don't
> catch more too, there won't be any left for us." So everyone starts
> overfishing. Within a year, the fish population collapses and nobody has
> any fish at all.
>
> This same principle applies to many environmental problems today:
> overfishing in the oceans, overuse of groundwater, deforestation, and
> even carbon emissions. Each country or company thinks, "If I reduce my
> emissions but others don't, I'll be at an economic disadvantage." So
> nobody reduces, and the shared atmosphere suffers.
>
> The solutions Hardin proposed were either privatization — dividing the
> commons so each person manages their own portion — or government
> regulation. But later economists, notably Elinor Ostrom, who won the
> Nobel Prize in 2009, showed that communities can successfully manage
> shared resources through cooperation, establishing rules and monitoring
> systems without needing government intervention.

**Pregunta Task 3:**
> Using the professor's examples, explain the concept of the tragedy of
> the commons and describe the solutions that have been proposed.

## TASK 4: Integrated — Academic Lecture + Speak (Preparación: 20s | Respuesta: 60s)

**Listening passage (usa TTS):**

> **Professor:** Today I want to talk about biomimicry — the practice of
> designing technology and solving human problems by imitating patterns
> found in nature. This is a rapidly growing field, and some of the results
> are quite fascinating.
>
> One classic example involves the Japanese bullet train, the Shinkansen.
> Engineers had a problem: every time the train exited a tunnel at high
> speed, it created a loud sonic boom that disturbed residents nearby. An
> engineer who was also a birdwatcher noticed that kingfisher birds dive
> from air into water — a transition between two very different densities
> — without creating a splash. He redesigned the front of the train to
> mimic the shape of a kingfisher's beak. The result? The train was not
> only quieter but also 10 percent faster and used 15 percent less energy.
>
> Another example comes from architecture. Termite mounds in Africa
> maintain a nearly constant internal temperature of 30 degrees Celsius,
> even when external temperatures fluctuate between 2 and 40 degrees. They
> achieve this through a system of vents and channels that circulate air
> naturally. Architects studied these mounds and designed the Eastgate
> Centre in Zimbabwe, a large office building that uses no conventional air
> conditioning. Instead, it uses a ventilation system inspired by termite
> mounds, reducing energy consumption for climate control by 90 percent
> compared to similar buildings.
>
> These examples show that nature has already solved many of the
> engineering and design problems we face. Billions of years of evolution
> have produced incredibly efficient solutions. Biomimicry simply asks:
> what can we learn from them?

**Pregunta Task 4:**
> Using the examples from the lecture, explain what biomimicry is and how
> it has been applied to solve real-world engineering problems.
