**Lecture 1: Introduction to the Carbon Cycle**

> Today I want to give you an overview of the carbon cycle — one of the most
> important biogeochemical cycles on Earth. Understanding how carbon moves
> through our planet's systems is fundamental to understanding climate change.
>
> Carbon exists in several reservoirs. The largest is the deep ocean, which
> holds about 37,000 gigatons of carbon. The atmosphere currently contains
> around 870 gigatons, and the terrestrial biosphere — that's all the plants,
> animals, and soil on land — holds roughly 2,000 gigatons.
>
> Now, carbon moves between these reservoirs through several processes. Plants
> absorb carbon dioxide from the atmosphere during photosynthesis. They convert
> CO2 and water into glucose and oxygen. When animals eat plants, the carbon
> transfers to them. When organisms die and decompose, carbon returns to the
> soil. Some of this carbon is eventually buried deep underground over millions
> of years, forming fossil fuels — coal, oil, and natural gas.
>
> Here's where human activity comes in. When we burn fossil fuels for energy,
> we're essentially taking carbon that was stored underground for millions of
> years and releasing it into the atmosphere as CO2 in a very short period of
> time. Before the Industrial Revolution, atmospheric CO2 was about 280 parts
> per million. Today, it's over 420 parts per million. That's a 50 percent
> increase, and most of it has occurred in just the last 70 years.
>
> The ocean absorbs about 25 percent of the CO2 we emit, which helps slow
> atmospheric warming but causes another problem: ocean acidification. When CO2
> dissolves in seawater, it forms carbonic acid, which lowers the pH of the
> water. This makes it harder for marine organisms like corals and shellfish to
> build their calcium carbonate shells and skeletons.
>
> So you can see that disrupting the carbon cycle has consequences that extend
> far beyond just warming temperatures. It affects ocean chemistry, marine life,
> and ultimately the entire food chain. For next class, I want you to read
> Chapter 7, which covers the nitrogen cycle, and think about how it interacts
> with what we discussed today.

**Lecture 2: Sustainable Campus Design**

> Good morning, everyone. Today we're going to look at a topic that's quite
> relevant to your daily lives: sustainable campus design. Universities around
> the world are becoming laboratories for green building practices, and our own
> campus is part of that trend.
>
> Let me start with energy. The average university campus in the United States
> spends between 15 and 30 million dollars per year on energy costs. That's a
> significant expense, and much of it comes from heating, cooling, and lighting
> buildings that were designed decades ago without energy efficiency in mind.
> Modern sustainable design addresses this through several strategies.
>
> First, there's passive solar design. This means orienting buildings so that
> they receive maximum sunlight in winter for natural heating and minimum direct
> sunlight in summer to reduce cooling costs. Large south-facing windows with
> thermal mass — like concrete floors that absorb heat during the day and
> release it at night — can reduce heating costs by up to 40 percent.
>
> Second, green roofs. A green roof is essentially a layer of vegetation
> planted on top of a building. It provides natural insulation, reducing both
> heating and cooling needs. It also absorbs rainwater, which reduces storm
> water runoff — a major cause of urban flooding and water pollution.
> Additionally, green roofs create habitat for insects and birds right in the
> middle of urban areas.
>
> Third, and this is something our campus implemented last year, is a greywater
> recycling system. Greywater is the relatively clean wastewater from sinks,
> showers, and laundry — not from toilets. Instead of sending all that water
> to the treatment plant, a greywater system filters and reuses it for
> irrigation and toilet flushing. Our campus system saves approximately
> 2 million gallons of fresh water per year.
>
> Now, the challenge with all of these approaches is upfront cost. A green
> roof costs about 25 to 50 dollars more per square foot than a conventional
> roof. However, studies show that the return on investment comes within 6 to
> 10 years through reduced energy and water costs, plus the building's lifespan
> is often extended by 20 years or more because the roof membrane is protected
> from UV radiation and temperature extremes.
>
> For your assignment, I'd like each group to choose one building on campus and
> propose three sustainable modifications, with a cost-benefit analysis for
> each. We'll present these in two weeks.
