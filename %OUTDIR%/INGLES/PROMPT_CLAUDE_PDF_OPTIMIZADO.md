LECTURE 1: The Carbon Cycle

Today I want to give you an overview of the carbon cycle — one of the most important biogeochemical cycles on Earth. Understanding how carbon moves through our planet's systems is fundamental to understanding climate change.

Carbon exists in several reservoirs. The largest is the deep ocean, which holds about 37,000 gigatons of carbon. The atmosphere currently contains around 870 gigatons, and the terrestrial biosphere — that's all the plants, animals, and soil on land — holds roughly 2,000 gigatons.

Now, carbon moves between these reservoirs through several processes. Plants absorb carbon dioxide from the atmosphere during photosynthesis. They convert CO2 and water into glucose and oxygen. When animals eat plants, the carbon transfers to them. When organisms die and decompose, carbon returns to the soil. Some of this carbon is eventually buried deep underground over millions of years, forming fossil fuels — coal, oil, and natural gas.

Here's where human activity comes in. When we burn fossil fuels for energy, we're essentially taking carbon that was stored underground for millions of years and releasing it into the atmosphere as CO2 in a very short period of time. Before the Industrial Revolution, atmospheric CO2 was about 280 parts per million. Today, it's over 420 parts per million. That's a 50 percent increase, and most of it has occurred in just the last 70 years.

The ocean absorbs about 25 percent of the CO2 we emit, which helps slow atmospheric warming but causes another problem: ocean acidification. When CO2 dissolves in seawater, it forms carbonic acid, which lowers the pH of the water. This makes it harder for marine organisms like corals and shellfish to build their calcium carbonate shells and skeletons.

So you can see that disrupting the carbon cycle has consequences that extend far beyond just warming temperatures. It affects ocean chemistry, marine life, and ultimately the entire food chain. For next class, I want you to read Chapter 7, which covers the nitrogen cycle, and think about how it interacts with what we discussed today.

LECTURE 2: Sustainable Campus Design

Good morning, everyone. Today we're going to look at a topic that's quite relevant to your daily lives: sustainable campus design. Universities around the world are becoming laboratories for green building practices, and our own campus is part of that trend.

Let me start with energy. The average university campus in the United States spends between 15 and 30 million dollars per year on energy costs. That's a significant expense, and much of it comes from heating, cooling, and lighting buildings that were designed decades ago without energy efficiency in mind. Modern sustainable design addresses this through several strategies.

First, there's passive solar design. This means orienting buildings so that they receive maximum sunlight in winter for natural heating and minimum direct sunlight in summer to reduce cooling costs. Large south-facing windows with thermal mass — like concrete floors that absorb heat during the day and release it at night — can reduce heating costs by up to 40 percent.

Second, green roofs. A green roof is essentially a layer of vegetation planted on top of a building. It provides natural insulation, reducing both heating and cooling needs. It also absorbs rainwater, which reduces storm water runoff — a major cause of urban flooding and water pollution. Additionally, green roofs create habitat for insects and birds right in the middle of urban areas.

Third, and this is something our campus implemented last year, is a greywater recycling system. Greywater is the relatively clean wastewater from sinks, showers, and laundry — not from toilets. Instead of sending all that water to the treatment plant, a greywater system filters and reuses it for irrigation and toilet flushing. Our campus system saves approximately 2 million gallons of fresh water per year.

Now, the challenge with all of these approaches is upfront cost. A green roof costs about 25 to 50 dollars more per square foot than a conventional roof. However, studies show that the return on investment comes within 6 to 10 years through reduced energy and water costs, plus the building's lifespan is often extended by 20 years or more because the roof membrane is protected from UV radiation and temperature extremes.

For your assignment, I'd like each group to choose one building on campus and propose three sustainable modifications, with a cost-benefit analysis for each. We'll present these in two weeks.

PASSAGE 1: The Water Cycle and Climate Change (Q 1-10)

The water cycle, also known as the hydrological cycle, describes the continuous movement of water within the Earth and its atmosphere. Water evaporates from oceans, lakes, and rivers, rises into the atmosphere as water vapor, condenses into clouds, and eventually falls back to the surface as precipitation — rain, snow, sleet, or hail. This cycle has been operating for billions of years and is essential for sustaining all life on Earth.

However, climate change is significantly altering the water cycle in ways that scientists are only beginning to fully understand. As global temperatures rise, the rate of evaporation increases. A warmer atmosphere can hold approximately 7 percent more moisture for every degree Celsius of warming. This means that when precipitation does occur, it tends to be heavier and more intense, leading to increased flooding in some regions.

Paradoxically, while some areas experience more intense rainfall, others are becoming drier. Warmer temperatures accelerate evaporation from soil and vegetation, and shifting weather patterns are redirecting moisture-carrying air currents away from some traditionally wet regions. The result is that droughts are becoming longer and more severe in many parts of the world, including the Mediterranean, southern Africa, and the western United States.

The implications for human societies are profound. Agriculture, which accounts for approximately 70 percent of global freshwater use, is particularly vulnerable. Farmers in drought-prone regions are being forced to adapt by switching to more drought-resistant crop varieties, improving irrigation efficiency, and in some cases, abandoning farming altogether. Meanwhile, cities in flood-prone areas are investing billions in infrastructure to manage increasingly severe storm events.

Scientists emphasize that these changes to the water cycle are not occurring in isolation. They interact with other effects of climate change — melting glaciers, rising sea levels, and shifting ecosystems — to create complex cascading impacts that are difficult to predict and even harder to reverse.

PASSAGE 2: The Evolution of University Education (Q 11-20)

The modern university bears little resemblance to its medieval origins. The first European universities, established in the 12th and 13th centuries in cities like Bologna, Paris, and Oxford, were essentially guilds of scholars — loosely organized groups of teachers and students who came together to study theology, law, medicine, and the liberal arts. There were no campuses, no standardized curricula, and no formal degrees in the modern sense. Students might study with a particular master for several years before being recognized as scholars themselves.

The transformation of universities began in earnest during the 19th century, particularly in Germany. Wilhelm von Humboldt's founding of the University of Berlin in 1810 introduced a revolutionary concept: the unity of teaching and research. Humboldt believed that professors should not merely transmit existing knowledge but actively create new knowledge through research, and that students should participate in this process. This model — where the university serves as both an educational institution and a research center — became the foundation for modern universities worldwide.

The 20th century brought perhaps the most dramatic expansion in university education's history. In the United States, the GI Bill of 1944 provided college funding for millions of returning World War II veterans, transforming higher education from an elite privilege into a mass institution. Before the war, only about 5 percent of Americans held college degrees. By 2000, that figure had risen to over 25 percent, and today it exceeds 35 percent.

This expansion has not been without controversy. Critics argue that the rapid growth of universities has led to degree inflation — the phenomenon where jobs that previously required only a high school diploma now demand a bachelor's degree, even when the actual work has not changed significantly. Others point to the dramatic rise in tuition costs, particularly in the United States, where student loan debt has surpassed 1.7 trillion dollars, creating a financial burden that shapes graduates' career choices and life decisions for decades after they leave campus.

Despite these challenges, the university remains one of society's most important institutions. Research conducted at universities has led to countless advances in medicine, technology, and our understanding of the natural world. Moreover, studies consistently show that college graduates earn significantly more over their lifetimes than those without degrees, suggesting that despite rising costs, a university education remains a worthwhile investment for most students.

PASSAGE 3: Photosynthesis and the Global Ecosystem (Q 21-30)

Photosynthesis is arguably the most important chemical process on Earth. This remarkable mechanism, carried out by plants, algae, and certain bacteria, converts light energy from the sun into chemical energy stored in glucose molecules. In doing so, it produces the oxygen that makes up approximately 21 percent of Earth's atmosphere and provides the foundation for nearly every food chain on the planet.

The process occurs in two main stages. The light-dependent reactions take place in the thylakoid membranes of chloroplasts, where chlorophyll — the green pigment in plants — absorbs light energy. This energy is used to split water molecules into hydrogen and oxygen atoms. The oxygen is released as a byproduct (the oxygen we breathe), while the hydrogen atoms are used to generate ATP and NADPH, two energy-carrying molecules. The second stage, called the Calvin cycle or light-independent reactions, occurs in the stroma of the chloroplast. Here, the ATP and NADPH from the first stage power the conversion of carbon dioxide from the atmosphere into glucose through a series of enzyme-catalyzed reactions.

What makes photosynthesis so critical to the global ecosystem extends far beyond its role in producing food and oxygen. Photosynthetic organisms are the primary mechanism by which carbon dioxide is removed from the atmosphere. Forests, grasslands, and ocean phytoplankton collectively absorb billions of tons of CO2 each year, acting as what scientists call "carbon sinks." Without this natural carbon capture, atmospheric CO2 levels would be dramatically higher, and global temperatures would be far more extreme than they are today.

However, human activities are undermining this natural balance. Deforestation eliminates millions of acres of photosynthetic capacity each year. When forests are burned or cleared, the carbon stored in trees is released back into the atmosphere, turning former carbon sinks into carbon sources. Meanwhile, ocean warming and acidification are reducing the productivity of marine phytoplankton, which are responsible for producing an estimated 50 to 80 percent of Earth's oxygen.

Scientists are exploring ways to enhance natural photosynthesis to combat climate change. Some researchers are working on genetically engineering crops with more efficient photosynthetic pathways, which could simultaneously increase food production and carbon capture. Others are developing artificial photosynthesis systems — devices that mimic the natural process to convert sunlight and CO2 into useful fuels. While these technologies are still in early stages, they represent promising approaches to addressing one of humanity's greatest challenges.

PASSAGE 4: Sleep and Academic Performance (Q 31-40)

Among the many factors that influence academic success, sleep is perhaps the most underestimated. Research over the past two decades has consistently demonstrated a strong correlation between sleep quality, duration, and cognitive performance — yet university students remain one of the most sleep-deprived populations in developed countries.

A 2023 study published in the Journal of Educational Psychology surveyed over 5,000 university students across 14 countries and found that the average student sleeps only 6.2 hours per night during the academic year, well below the 7 to 9 hours recommended by sleep scientists. More alarmingly, 35 percent of students reported sleeping fewer than 6 hours on most nights, a level associated with significant cognitive impairment.

The relationship between sleep and learning is rooted in neuroscience. During sleep, the brain consolidates memories — transferring information from short-term to long-term storage through a process that occurs primarily during deep sleep and REM (rapid eye movement) sleep stages. Students who sleep fewer than 6 hours show measurably poorer performance on memory recall tests, problem-solving tasks, and critical thinking exercises compared to those who sleep 7 or more hours.

Furthermore, sleep deprivation affects the prefrontal cortex, the brain region responsible for executive functions such as planning, decision-making, and attention regulation. A sleep-deprived student may sit through an entire lecture without retaining any meaningful information — not because the material is too difficult, but because their brain literally lacks the resources to encode new information.

Some universities have begun to address this issue institutionally. Several schools have eliminated classes that begin before 9 a.m., and others have created "nap rooms" in libraries and student centers. Stanford University's Sleep Medicine Center has developed a program that teaches students sleep hygiene techniques — consistent bedtime routines, limited screen time before sleep, and strategic use of short naps — which has been shown to improve both sleep quality and GPA among participants.

The irony of student sleep deprivation is that many students sacrifice sleep to study more, believing that additional hours of study will improve their grades. However, research shows the opposite: students who maintain consistent sleep schedules perform better academically than those who engage in marathon study sessions at the expense of rest. As one sleep researcher put it, "You cannot pour knowledge into a brain that is too tired to receive it."

PASSAGE 5: Coral Reef Ecosystems Under Threat (Q 41-50)

Coral reefs, sometimes called the "rainforests of the sea," are among the most biologically diverse ecosystems on Earth. Although they cover less than 1 percent of the ocean floor, they support an estimated 25 percent of all marine species, including over 4,000 species of fish, 800 species of hard corals, and countless invertebrates, algae, and microorganisms. Beyond their ecological value, coral reefs provide essential services to human communities, including coastal protection from storms, food for hundreds of millions of people, and economic benefits through fishing and tourism estimated at 375 billion dollars annually.

Despite their importance, coral reefs worldwide are in serious decline. The primary threat is ocean warming caused by climate change. Corals have a symbiotic relationship with tiny algae called zooxanthellae, which live within coral tissues and provide up to 90 percent of the coral's energy through photosynthesis. When ocean temperatures rise even slightly — typically just 1 to 2 degrees Celsius above the normal summer maximum — corals become stressed and expel their zooxanthellae. This process, known as coral bleaching, leaves the coral white and starving. If temperatures return to normal quickly, corals can recover. However, prolonged or repeated bleaching events lead to coral death.

The 2016-2017 global bleaching event was the most severe ever recorded, affecting approximately 75 percent of the world's tropical reefs. The Great Barrier Reef in Australia lost nearly 30 percent of its shallow-water corals in a single year. Scientists warn that if global temperatures rise by 2 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels — a threshold that could be reached by mid-century — virtually all tropical coral reefs will experience annual severe bleaching, making recovery essentially impossible.

Ocean acidification poses an additional threat. As the ocean absorbs more CO2 from the atmosphere, its pH decreases, making it more acidic. This reduced pH makes it harder for corals to build their calcium carbonate skeletons — the rigid structures that form the physical foundation of the reef. Studies have shown that coral calcification rates have already declined by approximately 15 percent since pre-industrial times.

Conservation efforts are underway but face enormous challenges. Marine protected areas can reduce local stressors such as overfishing and pollution, but they cannot protect reefs from warming oceans and acidification — problems that require global solutions. Some scientists are experimenting with "assisted evolution," selectively breeding corals that show greater heat tolerance, and transplanting them onto degraded reefs. Others are creating coral gene banks — frozen repositories of coral genetic material — as insurance against future loss.

The fate of coral reefs ultimately depends on humanity's ability to limit global warming. As marine biologist Dr. Ruth Gates once stated, "Coral reefs are the canary in the coal mine for climate change. If we cannot save the reefs, we cannot save ourselves."
